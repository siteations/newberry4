    1. 1 media/case_3a_643_tp_o3_cropped.jpg 2017-06-07T16:50:19+00:00 Keeping the Clergy in Line 14 plain 2017-07-17T17:51:24+00:00
      Laypeople complained about the clergy even in the medieval church, but print gave their criticisms an entirely different feel beginning in the 16th century, especially in Protestant lands. Clergy in these areas faced a much more empowered even aggressive laity that was always ready to vent its frustration through print.

      Luther had insisted that congregations had the power to call (and remove) their pastors, and this power became a standard feature in most Protestant churches. This idea encouraged laity to devote more energy to holding their spiritual leaders accountable. In many ways, this made it more difficult for a pastor to do his work, since he could not take his congregations respect and obedience for granted. Works of pastoral advice were sure to address this issue, offering tips for spiritual leaders to remain on their parishioners good side without compromising their integrity. For instance, the Anglican George Herberts short tract A Priest to the Temple, recommends that parsons working in rural parishes avoid arousing the contempt of their parishioners by living a blameless life, maintaining a courteous and amiable manner, and justly (but not excessively) correcting even of the best in the Parish.

      Print also made lay criticism of clergy (even the high clergy) much more effective than ever before. Anonymous short works like pamphlets or broadsides could do great harm to the clergy by swaying public opinion against them. The Anglican bishops faced precisely this problem in the late 16th century, when a series of satirical pamphlets written under the pseudonym Martin Marprelate condemned the bishops for their hypocrisy and authoritarian ways. The Marprelate tracts were so popular that the bishops decided to pay authors to write pamphlets defending them, a clear recognition of the damage public opinion could do to the clerical office. One of the authors they employed was Thomas Nash, who adopted Martins satirical style in his own work. Nash took that style further in his wildly successful Pierce Penilesse, His Supplication to the Devil, a work that cemented his status as the leading pamphleteer in England in the 1590s.
