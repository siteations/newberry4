    1. 1 media/case_w_9111_611_plt_clxix_o3_cropped.jpg 2017-06-07T16:43:44+00:00 Clerical Challenges after the Reformation 31 image_header 2017-07-19T15:50:53+00:00 Detail from Adnotationes et meditationes in Euangelia quae in sacrosancto Missae sacrificio toto anno leguntur by Gerónimo Nadal. Antwerp: Martin Nuyts, 1595.
      After the outbreak of the Reformation, the basic task of ministry became dramatically more complicated. New churches, previously unknown populations, and the continuing search for clarity and order added ever more stress to the clerical office. Recognizing this, religious leaders created an array of publications to provide much-needed guidance for themselves and their spiritual flocks.
       
      The clergy who faced perhaps the highest degree of difficulty were missionaries to the Americas, who were both bearers of the Gospel and agents of colonization. Converting indigenous peoples required these men to master indigenous languages  to do their work. They also needed copies of spiritual texts in indigenous languages, and they needed them quickly. This meant making them by hand, since at first no presses were available in the Americas. Liturgical manuscripts like this Nahuatl Lectionary are evidence of missionaries’ methods of converting members of unfamiliar cultures. They simultaneously record the multiple challenges clergy faced in converting indigenous peoples and also the furthering of colonization of the Americas.  The use of local languages to teach Christianity led to a wide range of results, from achieving more converts to syncretism between Christianity and indigenous spiritual beliefs to the practice of traditional indigenous religious culture in secret.
      Back in Europe, clergy in the new Protestant churches faced a daunting challenge of their own. Unlike their medieval predecessors, they were expected to be good theologians, convincing preachers, and effective administrators as well as virtuous ministers. The first generations of Protestant pastors often lacked one or more of these skills, forcing them to learn the rest on the job. This was a heavy burden for even the most prepared clergyman, so Lutheran leaders developed reference books called church orders, which included everything a pastor needed to know to effectively lead his church (doctrines, readings, worship instructions, etc.). These books were often marked up, and the annotations reveal pastors learning how to be good Lutherans along with their parishioners. This may have been the experience of the owner of the Church Order…for the County of Mecklenburg, who found it necessary to highlight in his copy one of the essential doctrines of Luther’s church – that salvation comes only from faith in Christ.
      Catholic clergy in Europe also found their work more challenging than did their medieval predecessors, since they had to split their time between ministering to their flock and defending it from Protestantism. The latter proved difficult, since Protestants were in the early decades often better at producing texts for general audiences. To counteract this advantage, Catholic clergy often relied more heavily on images to achieve their goals of conversion and spiritual guidance. The Jesuits led the way in this effort, putting images to work in a variety of publications that trained Catholic clergy. The Comments and Meditations on the Gospel is a prime example of this approach; Jesuit priests carried it with them wherever they went in the world to supply their converts and their students with images from the life of Christ to use in their devotional activities. In this way, Jesuits hoped to both enrich Catholics’ relationship with God and turn them away from Protestant propaganda.
