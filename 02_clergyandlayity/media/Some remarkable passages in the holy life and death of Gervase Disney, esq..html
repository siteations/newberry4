    1. 1 media/case_wing_zc_93_185_fl_026_003_o3_cropped.jpg 2017-06-07T16:47:52+00:00 Print and The Priesthood of All Believers 25 gallery 2017-07-17T17:47:15+00:00 Saint Mary, Blessed Virgin, Annuciation [Italian Religious Broadsides collection]. 1660.
      Martin Luther had controversially claimed that everyone belonged to the priesthood of all believers, which obliterated the sharp boundary that had existed between clergy and laity in the medieval Church. The impact was unmistakable; even in areas that did not adopt Protestantism, the laity began to take on more of the roles that had once been the unique privilege of the clergy, such as practicing public religion, teaching doctrine, and modeling virtue.
      A focal point of lay participation that continued unabated from the Middle Ages was the religious festival. Confraternities, local religious associations that were associated with guilds, organized public celebrations and commemorations for religious feast days that were open to the general public, usually without the direct involvement of the clergy. The festivals were advertised by religious broadsides, inexpensive posters that featured reprints of famous woodcuts and engravings, decorative borders, and poems composed just for the occasion. These rare objects are powerful illustrations of how printers, artists, and lay patrons came together to quickly create what they needed for their public religion without the assistance of the clergy.
      Among Protestants, the home became one of the most important locations for religious education. Following the example of Luther and his wife, Protestant fathers and mothers had the responsibility to give their children and their entire family establishment (including servants) a firm footing in every aspect of Christianity, from moral behavior to familiarity with Christian doctrine. This was no small undertaking, so religious thinkers created a host of publications (catechisms, pamphlets, primers) to make this as easy as possible. Robert Abbots Milk for Babes, for instance, arranged the chief saving principles of Christian religion into a simple question-and-answer format that mothers of all skill levels could use to instruct their children.
      As their spiritual responsibilities increased, laypeople began to look to each other for examples of virtuous Christian behavior, rather than exclusively to saints or clergymen. Some lay role models were enshrined in spiritual autobiographies rather than in church-based images or pilgrimage sites. In these books, devout laypeople described their experience of spiritual triumphs and struggles for the benefit of the audience. To do this, they promoted a close bond between the subject and the reader, in the hopes that the latter might come to salvation by following or at least identifying with the life of the former as much as possible. For instance, readers of Some Remarkable Passages in the Holy Life of Gervase Disney would find a list of the lessons Disney learned from his spiritual afflictions that could help them avoid such difficulties in the future.
