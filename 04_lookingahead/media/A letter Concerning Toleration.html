    1. 1 2017-07-10T19:53:39+00:00 Religious Tolerance 3 plain 2017-07-18T14:28:37+00:00
      The religious changes of the early modern period eventually redefined how people thought about religion and its role in society and public life. The violence and acrimony of the battle over “true” Christianity created palpable tension with the central Christian tenets of mercy, forgiveness, and peace. These tensions only grew as the efforts to impose one form of Christianity over others increased in ferocity, and by 1700 more and more voices had been raised in favor of  breaking that tension through religious tolerance.
      There had been calls for religious tolerance since the early days of the Reformation, but they largely went unheeded by religious and secular authorities until the traumatic experience of the French Wars of Religion and especially the Thirty Years’ War. The latter, with its extraordinary destructiveness, served as a dramatic wake-up call, and those in authority began to listen more closely to assertions that being a good Christian was something different from being a good subject or citizen.
      One of the most significant voices making this distinction belonged to the English philosopher John Locke, who, like many others, looked for ways to diminish or even cease the religious rancor that had torn whole nations apart, including his own. His solution, which he first articulated publicly (but anonymously) in A Letter Concerning Toleration, was to separate the state from the practice of religion. Religion, he suggested, was a matter of conscience, and since “Liberty of conscience is every mans natural Right…no body ought to be compelled in matters of Religion either by Law or by Force.”
      Locke’s concept of religious toleration was hardly absolute (he denied that atheists or even Catholics should be tolerated), but his argument that the state had no authority intervening in Protestant religious beliefs or practices proved a compelling one. It and its Dutch counterparts were developed further over the next century, most notably in the fledgling United States, where the framers of its Constitution used them to found, for the first time, a nation in which church and state were formally separated.
