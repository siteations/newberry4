    During the 16th and 17th centuries, the split between Catholics and Protestants dominated European life. At the turn of the 18th  century, these groups were perhaps no closer to resolving their differences, but the intensity of their debate tended to wane as the Reformation receded more and more into historical memory. Religious change was becoming religious history, and people used the artifacts of the earlier spiritual struggles for new purposes.

    By 1700, differentiating Protestantism from Catholicism was starting to become a subject for academic study, rather than being purely a battle for the soul of Christendom. Publications from the 16th century transitioned from urgent weapons into historical sources for particular religious identities. We can see evidence of that transition in this copy of Martin Chemnitzs Examination of the Council of Trent. Originally written as a Lutheran attack on Catholic theology and practice, this book entered into the libraries of Protestant seminaries, where aspiring pastors used it to learn the theological particulars of their church. The copious annotations inside show generations of students using it for less polemical purposes than Chemnitz had originally intended.

    As time passed, the events and ideas associated with the Reformation became fodder for more popular fare, including humor and satire. This may well have been the purpose of the enigmatic Confession of the King of Poland, a broadside that paired an image of Martin Luther and the pope with an over-the-top pro-Lutheran confession. The bizarre nature of this piece suggests that it was a satirical take on the Protestant-Catholic debate, perhaps even an effort to make light of how seriously people in the past took the dispute. Whatever its purpose, the broadsheet shows how the figures of Luther and the pope had become symbols that could be used to do different kinds of cultural work once they were removed from their original context.
