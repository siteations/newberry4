    The breakdown of the cultural unity provided by the medieval church led to a dramatic increase in international war and violence. Whereas medieval rulers were theoretically bound by a common understanding of divine law that was determined by the church, early modern rulers were freer to define divine law on their own terms, and thus were more likely to look to their own interests alone.

    The increasing prevalence of war was obvious to all early modern people, and many recognized the loss of a unifying religious foundation as a root cause. Increasingly, academics endeavored to restore that unity with something less controversial than religion. Their efforts accelerated the development of the concept of international relations. One of the most important theorists to participate in this process was the Dutch intellectual Hugo Grotius, whose treatise The Illustrious Hugo Grotius Of the law of Warre and Peace offered a much different approach to how sovereigns should treat one another.
    Grotius presented a more collaborative vision of international relations, in which states abided by a common set of principles that promoted cooperation and mediation in order to secure common good. His experience of the tumultuous religious conflicts of his day clearly played a role in his view. For instance, when discussing the concept of a just war, Grotius listed only three acceptable justifications for a military action against another power: defense, recovery of property, and inflicting punishment. Religion is conspicuously absent from this list, an indication of the widespread attempt to find the more practical basis for international interaction that came to fruition only in the latter half of the 20th century.
