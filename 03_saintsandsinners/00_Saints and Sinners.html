    Saints and Sinners

    1 2017-02-10T15:54:08+00:00 Newberry Library 09980eb76a145ec4f3814f3b9fb45f381b3d1f02 3 6 plain 2121 2017-07-18T14:31:37+00:00 Newberry Library 09980eb76a145ec4f3814f3b9fb45f381b3d1f02
    The events of the early Reformation began the most intense public relations campaign Europe had ever known. Early modern faith communities worked feverishly to convince as many people as possible to join (or stay with) them. These efforts involved both building up their own reputations and smearing those of others by controlling a particularly powerful narrative with a pair of thematic questions: who are the saints, and who are the sinners?
    According to this narrative, individuals (members of elites as well as ordinary people) were depicted as either true disciples of God or agents of the devil. At its core was the need to shape public perception of the religious changes underway in the early modern period. Major religious events or developments were rarely discussed objectively; all had to be spun into proof that God or the devil was somehow at work in them. 
    The European public had an insatiable desire for this kind of narrative. People were eager to hear or read about the divine implications of important events in settings and formats that were readily accessible; not just preachers but writers and printers were happy to give the public what it wanted. The items here show a sampling of the strategies and approaches churches employed to tell a story in print better than the competition.
