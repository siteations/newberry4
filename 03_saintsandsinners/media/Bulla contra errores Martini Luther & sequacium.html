    1. 1 2017-06-21T20:57:28+00:00 Spinning the Reformation 5 plain 2017-07-18T14:32:50+00:00
      The public relations battle over saints and sinners began almost immediately after Martin Luther circulated his 95 Theses. His challenge to the sale of indulgences drew a quick response from local indulgence preachers, who recognized the potential fallout of Luther’s ideas – perhaps even before he himself did.

      The papacy’s official response came in 1520 with the Bull Against the Errors of Martin Luther, a papal edict that was meant to be the definitive public statement on the Wittenberg professor and his ideas. It purpose was to convince all Christians in Germany that Luther was a dangerous heretic, and to that end it formally condemned 41 of Luther’s ideas as heresy, commanded the burning of his books, and forbade him from preaching. The gravity of the situation was obvious from its first words, “Arise, Lord, and declare Your own cause,” which was, readers would find, not Luther’s.

      Efforts were made to ensure that the bull reached as wide an audience as possible. Official broadsheet copies were distributed to major cathedrals in Germany, but printers also produced a pamphlet version like the edition shown here, which almost immediately became a bestseller throughout Europe. In the end, however, the bull was unable to overcome the extensive public support Luther built through his own print campaign, but it was a sign of the religious public relations war to come.
