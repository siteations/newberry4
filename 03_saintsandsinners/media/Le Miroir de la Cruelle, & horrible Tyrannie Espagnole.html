    1. 1 media/ayer_108_c64_1620m_tp_o2_cropped.jpg 2017-06-21T21:22:33+00:00 Religious Propaganda 9 image_header 2017-07-19T15:59:07+00:00 Detail from Le Miroir de la Cruelle, & horrible Tyrannie Espagnole by Bartholomé de las Casas. Amsterdam: Jan Evertszoon Cloppenburg, 1620.
      Religious changes were extremely complicated, but the competing Christian churches did their best make them appear as simple as possible. Controlling the narrative of saints and sinnerdepended in large part on disseminating an accessible and compelling interpretation of religious events as soon as possible after they had taken place. In modern terms, this was propaganda, and it played an essential role in the public relations battles of the early modern period.
      Religious propagandists used all genres at their disposal to broadcast their chosen narrative. These included music, which was widely recognized as an effective way to instill ideas in society at large. An example of musical religious propaganda is A Song Made for the Landsknechts, a brief musical pamphlet marketed specifically to the foot soldiers fighting for the Lutheran German princes against the Catholic armies of Charles V during the Schmalkaldic War. Set to simple melodies, the song justified the Protestant cause and motivated the soldiers to support it. In addition to stressing the religious differences between the two sides, the song also depicted imperial forces as tyrannical monsters bent on stealing German lands and goods.
      Propagandists were always on the lookout for any piece of news or any event that could help them control the narrative of saints and sinners. One of the more illuminating examples of this tendency is the so-called Black Legend, the term given to the mountain of (mostly Protestant) publications painting the kingdom of Spain as the epitome of diabolical evil. Spain gave its enemies plenty of grist for their propaganda mills with its harsh repression of Protestants in the Low Countries and brutal subjugation of indigenous communities in the Americas.
      One of the cornerstones of the Black Legend was the Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies by Bartholomé de las Casas, an account of the atrocities against Central and South American Indians committed by Spanish conquistadors. Originally written to convince the king of Spain to end the colonial violence, Las Casas’ text was especially popular with Protestants, who published translated editions to demonstrate the depths of Spanish cruelty. The edition shown here is a French translation printed in the Protestant Netherlands during that country’s bitter war for independence from their Catholic rulers. Typically for these translations, this edition included illustrations to drive home the inhumanity of the Spanish against indigenous communities. The engraving here shows conquistadores horrifically murdering innocent Quiché people from Guatemala, including children and pregnant women.
